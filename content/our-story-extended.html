The situations that shaped SafetyNet:

  • Bad dates: Stuck in unsafe situations for hours, but no one knew there was a problem
  • Stolen phones: Sudden radio silence that looked like "just being busy."
  • The spiked drink: A friend compromised on a business trip in a nearby city, no one knew to look for him
  • The false assumption: Friends held hostage in an Airbnb for days — everyone assumed they were safe because they were "together."
  • The silent failure: Travelers went missing on another continent, but only their parents realized, after a few days.
  • The silent accident: A colleague slipped while hiking alone. No one knew she was in trouble until it was too late.
  • The delayed search: Tourists lost in the bush often weren't reported missing for days.
  • The missing context: Even when reported, search teams had no starting point to find them quickly.
  • The solo fear: "If I stopped posting on social media, how long would it take for anyone to notice?"
  • The verification problem: Parents worrying about children abroad without a way to verify their safety without nagging.
  • The unfamiliar country problem: "How would anyone back home know what to do, what steps to take?"

The pattern was clear:

Everyone assumed they were fine until they weren't. No one knew there was a problem, what the context was, or what to do.
